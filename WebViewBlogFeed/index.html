Say NO to No Shave November !
Say NO to No Shave November !
19 Jan

November, the month of lights, holidays and of course, no shaving. It is the month, where men start off with smooth, baby cheeks and end up looking much similar to a chimpanzee, or the likes. So what is 'No shave November'?


There are possibly two ways to answer this question.


Ask a teenager, and he will ignorantly tell you about it being the month to Snapchat daily images of progressively growing beards, or God forbid, tweet #LetItGrow, for a duration or 30 days, without once ever stopping to think about the whys and the whats. Many would dare to say it is for cancer, but have you ever stopped to ponder over how your blessed moustache would do any good in the world?


The act of not shaving a whole month was essentially started to create awareness of men's health issues, such as depression in men, prostate cancer and other male cancers, and associated charities. The movement is a way for us, to aid cancer patients in their struggles by refraining from grooming our beards, and instead directing that money to cancer associations, like the American Cancer society.


This is not only limited to men. Women can undeniably do their bit, by skipping a visit to the salon and instead donating this money to cancer associations. The cause albeit noble, is being overlooked by us people as being irrelevant, as opposed to its social importance. The herd mentality of blindly following the latest fad has reached such high levels of absurdity, where teenagers are willing to throw buckets full of ice cold water on themselves, without once giving a hoot about the social significance of such, otherwise ludicrous acts. Regardless of whether you shave or not, pitch in a little, support the moment and donate. Participate in the moment with the complete knowledge of how one small act can create an avalanche effect. After all, growing a 'mo' on its own, never helped no patient.

The High Heeled Life
The High Heeled Life
26 Jan

I must have been 6 or 7, gleefully trying out my elder sister's and mother's heels, impatiently waiting for the day I'd get to wear those beauties for real.


Fast forward 12 years, and I'm teetering on those very heels in the most unladylike fashion, heading off in unintended directions to maintain my balance, and also living in constant paranoia of spraining an ankle or stepping on some poor soul's foot!


So why are women all over the world forsaking their comfort and a pain free life for the high heel? And which smart alec came up with the idea in the first place?


It may surprise you, but the high heel was originally meant for men. It was an instrument of war rather than seduction (come to think of it, they can still be used to direly cause hurt).


15th century Persian soldiers used the heel to secure their feet in stirrups for greater leverage with their bow and arrow as they went galloping into battle (by the end of the sixteenth century, the Persian army was the largest in the world). As Persian messengers traveled to Europe, they took their heels with them, which immediately caught the fancy of European male aristocrats. They realized wearing high heels prevented their feet from coming into contact with say, horse droppings and other muck on the streets of the Renaissance city. And oh, it made them look so much taller and intimidating. The heel was not meant to be pretty, it was meant to be scary! (they still are by the way) By the 18th century, heels were one of the biggest power symbols until the French revolution, when democracy became the new fashion. They were completely done away with, until the 20th century when porn photographers realized that heels made women's bodies look great! Then, there was no looking back.


Technology, ever since, has made heels more stable and easier to wear (hahaha). And although they put a lot of stress on the lower back, cause bunions and painful bone deformities of the foot with excess use and cause embarrassing falls at times, they make a woman feel beautiful, more powerful and confident. Living the high heeled life requires skill and panache. After all, they aren't called killer heels for nothing!

It is no nation we inhabit, but a Culture.
"It is no nation we inhabit, but a Culture" - Skull Face, Metal Gear Solid V
11 Feb

Manto, one of the greatest writers the world would never know once wrote this about Bombay, 'You can be happy here on two pennies a day or on ten thousand rupees a day, if you wish. You can also spend your life here as the unhappiest man in the world. You can do what you want. No one will find fault with you. Nor will anyone subject you to moralizing. You alone will have to accomplish the most difficult of tasks and you alone will have to make the most difficult decision of your life. You may live on the footpath or in a magnificent palace. If it never knew you came, it'll never know you left.'


Strangers, impossibilities, opportunity and collapse brush shoulders, tomorrow can bring anything. Wealth, insanity, anything. And yesterday is an ancient as 1700. Perhaps it is this idea of anything happening tomorrow that keeps Mumbaikars running.


A friend of mine who Studies in Aachen, Germany says for Days, sometimes weeks, she can tell what her next day will be like - and it will be exactly like the previous one. There's a system, a routine, the local Breakfast diner, her College, the morning train at 8:47. Yes, there's change in her Life, but nothing that isn't foreseen.


There's her Assignments, the semester exams, the chocolate indulgence on the commute home, the occasional party she attends. Excluding acts of god, freakish weather and an emergency or two, life has a pattern - a known system. The train comes on time, her hostel in some quiet leafy suburb has no infrastructure collapse daily. The next 2 years, even though they can't be predicted, can be assumed.


I heard this and was in awe. Not because I thought this was some great life (it seemed a bit boring actually), but when I heard of the repetition of everyday, it was like someone was telling me about Santa Claus for the first time. As someone who has lived in Mumbai all his life, I've never had one minute the same as the next, let alone days. We couldn't predict the next 11 seconds, let alone the next few years. Nobody, not one person, in the world lives like us, Tribals in the amazon who live and hunt in the trees seem 9 to 5 compared to us.


Isaac Asimov in his many predictions once said that there would be a society in the future, if taken out of their comfort zone of chaos, would not know how to socially function in other environments.


As Mumbaikars, we're it.
